\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Context:} Node.js/Express services reach relational databases
through a spectrum of access layers (native drivers, query builders, and
object-relational mappers, or ORMs), yet practitioners choose between them largely
on vendor benchmarks whose numbers are rarely shown to be comparable --- fragmented,
PostgreSQL-only, and seldom reporting the tail (p99).
\textbf{Objective:} We contribute a reusable \emph{comparability protocol} for
access-layer benchmarking --- a semantic-equivalence correctness gate, a
documentation-selected estimand bounded by a same-SQL control, and separated
operating points --- realized in an open harness and demonstrated through a
dual-engine PostgreSQL/MySQL case study. The protocol, not any ranking, is the
contribution.
\textbf{Methods:} An identical Express application is served through nine documentation-selected
access layers (native drivers \texttt{pg} and \texttt{mysql2}, the query builder
Knex, and the ORMs Drizzle, Prisma, Sequelize, TypeORM, Objection and MikroORM) and
two tuned native baselines, over five access patterns, on the latest stable
library versions current at the July 2026 freeze. We report throughput with client-observed response-time p99 from
25 repeated runs per cell under three operating conditions: equal demand (a
fixed 50-connection high-load point) across all patterns, and---on the deep fetch only---equal
utilization (an open-loop sweep) and an exploratory equal compute budget; an automated
cross-check verifies byte-identical responses
before measurement, and inserts run under the engines' default durability.
\textbf{Results:} The implementation-and-strategy difference concentrates in
relation-materializing patterns: the deep/nested fetch spreads $7.15\times$
(PostgreSQL) and $4.96\times$ (MySQL) between native and the slowest ORM,
against only $1.68\times$ and $2.01\times$ among the raw paths running common SQL, a
compound control that bounds the combined query-strategy-and-formulation
contribution without isolating a single mechanism. The read ordering holds
across engines (Spearman $\rho \geq 0.86$), but the aggregation and insert orderings
reverse: Prisma drops to last on the MySQL insert, and the per-layer engine
advantage scatters $1.6$--$3.2\times$. No layer exceeds
$\approx\!110\%$ of one core; the native driver leads all ten engine-pattern
combinations and Prisma~7 sits mid-pack.
\textbf{Conclusion:} The case study shows why the protocol matters: access-layer
rankings are configuration-specific and version-sensitive (one earlier five-core
result did not survive the re-run), so the durable contribution is the protocol and
its harness, not any single ranking.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Data availability}
The complete replication package (benchmark harness, database schema and
deterministic seed, all adapter implementations, the byte-level correctness cross-check,
raw per-cell measurements, and the scripts that generate every table and figure) is
available at \texttt{https://github.com/mmiotk/express-db-access-performance};
release v1.6.4 is permanently archived at Zenodo
(\texttt{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.21444624}). A reproducibility summary --- versions,
requirements, run commands, time and resources, and which results the harness regenerates
automatically from the archived raw data --- is in Supplement Table~S31 and
\texttt{REPRODUCE.md}. This is the generic build; the Elsevier submission build is
\texttt{ist/ist\_main.tex}.
