\begin{abstract}
Node.js/Express applications reach relational databases through a spectrum of
access layers: native drivers, query builders, and object-relational mappers
(ORMs). Practitioners choose between them largely on the basis of vendor
benchmarks, which are fragmented, PostgreSQL-only, and report throughput or
latency but rarely the tail (p99). We present a vendor-independent, reproducible benchmark of
a broad cross-section of the access-layer taxonomy's three tiers (native drivers
\texttt{pg} and \texttt{mysql2}, a query builder Knex, and six ORMs: Drizzle,
Prisma, Sequelize, TypeORM, Objection, and MikroORM, plus two tuned native
baselines), served through an identical
Express application over five access patterns, on both PostgreSQL and MySQL,
reporting throughput and tail latency (p99) together from 25 independent
replicates, with every layer verified to return byte-identical responses before
measurement. Abstraction cost concentrates in relation-materializing patterns:
the deep/nested fetch spreads $6.5\times$ (PostgreSQL) and $4.5\times$ (MySQL)
between the native driver and the heaviest data-mapper ORM, and the spread grows
with the size of the fetched graph; a same-SQL control attributes most of it to each
layer's query-generation and result-construction path (default eager-loading
strategy, query count, and object hydration) rather than to raw SQL execution. Read rankings transfer across engines; the write ranking does
not: under default durability, single-row inserts are engine-bound on MySQL but
layer-dependent on PostgreSQL. Prisma matches or leads native throughput on
several patterns at roughly five times the CPU of every other layer.
\end{abstract}
