\section*{Response to Reviewer (Revision Round 3)}

We thank the reviewer for a detailed and constructive report on the full manuscript. The recurring theme across review rounds has been that several statements were stronger than our design supports. We have taken that seriously and made a deliberate, paper-wide change of stance: \textbf{we claim less, and we claim it once.} The paper's spine is now \emph{whole-stack idiomatic performance under a stated operating point}, with the same-SQL control presented as a \textbf{contrast that bounds} the raw-execution component rather than a decomposition that isolates mechanisms. We also ran the four targeted experiments the review pointed toward. All section and table references below are to the revised submission build (\texttt{ist/ist\_main.tex}); a numbered online supplement (Supplement Tables S1--S20) accompanies it. Effective main-text length is 14,996 words, under the 15,000 limit (\texttt{word-count.pdf}).

\section*{Overarching change: claim less, decisively}

Wherever the manuscript previously suggested that the same-SQL control \emph{decomposes} the deficit or that "most of the cost lies upstream," it now states that the control \textbf{changes query generation, hydration, protocol, and mapping together as a bundle and isolates none of them individually}; it \emph{bounds} the raw-execution component. This wording is now consistent across both abstracts, the Introduction, the Methodology estimand naming (renamed "same-SQL (raw-path) estimand \ldots{} a compound contrast, not a mechanism-isolating decomposition"), Results, Discussion, and Conclusion.

\section*{Major concerns}

\textbf{\S{}6.1 --- same-SQL framing overclaimed.} Corrected as above. The estimand is now a compound contrast; Results and Discussion state explicitly that it does not attribute the deficit to any single component (Results \S{}"Same-SQL control"; Methodology \S{}"Analysis").

\textbf{\S{}6.2 --- p99 was under-qualified.} The primary p99 is now framed throughout as \textbf{saturated closed-loop tail latency at a 50-connection operating point against a ten-connection pool}, which includes connection-acquisition queueing (Abstract; Introduction; Methodology; Results \S{}"Tail latency"). As the intrinsic-tail companion we added a \textbf{coordinated-omission-corrected open-loop} measurement, and in this round extended it to \textbf{both engines} (new experiment; Supplement Table S17): the sub-saturation corrected tail is flat and small and each layer collapses once the offered rate crosses its capacity on MySQL as on PostgreSQL, so the intrinsic-tail ordering is engine-robust.

\textbf{\S{}6.3 --- "idiomatic" was underspecified.} We added a consolidated \texttt{experiments/METHODOLOGY.md} and a compact \textbf{Supplement Table S16} documenting, per adapter: the exact eager-loading API used on the deep fetch, its round-trip count, the documented alternative loading strategy we did \emph{not} use, and confirmation that query logging is disabled with no read-path hooks or validation (verified against source). We also ran an \textbf{alternative eager-loading sensitivity experiment} (Supplement Table S18): where the alternative is a byte-identical drop-in, switching the join-default data-mapper ORMs (Sequelize, MikroORM) to select-in makes them \emph{slower} (0.68--0.75$\times$), so their deficit is intrinsic rather than a poor default; conversely, Objection's batched-select-in default is 1.6$\times$ slower than a single join on MySQL, so part of \emph{its} deficit there is a strategy choice. TypeORM's query strategy errors on the ordered nested \texttt{findOne} in the pinned version and is disclosed as a portability caveat rather than measured.

\textbf{\S{}6.4 --- practical recommendations too broad.} Recommendations are scoped to layer class and access pattern and bounded by the operating point; we removed unconditional phrasings.

\textbf{\S{}6.5 --- Prisma mechanism conflated observation and inference.} We now separate the \textbf{observation} (Prisma draws \textasciitilde{}5$\times$ the application CPU at the fixed ten-connection pool; its ranking changes with concurrency) from the \textbf{documentation fact} (in-process multi-threaded query engine) from \textbf{inference}; the abstract CPU statement is qualified "at that operating point."

\textbf{\S{}6.6 --- MySQL insert mechanism asserted without direct evidence.} We ran the \textbf{performance\_schema commit-flush experiment} (new; Supplement Table S19). Sampling the redo-log and binary-log flush wait instruments during the insert workload at default durability, the flush takes 0.57--0.69 ms per insert (38--40\% of each insert) and is \textbf{nearly identical across the native driver, query builder, and slowest ORM} --- a shared engine floor --- and relaxing durability removes it and lifts throughput. The manuscript's "consistent with a shared MySQL-side commit cost" is now \emph{directly measured} (Results \S{}"RQ2"; Supplement \S{}"MySQL insert commit-flush mechanism").

\textbf{\S{}6.7 --- temporal concentration of measurements.} We added a \textbf{post-restart robustness check} (new; Supplement Table S20): after restarting both database engines (cold buffer pools, fresh connections) we re-measured a representative deep-fetch subset eight days after the primary campaign. The layer ranking reproduces on both engines; absolute throughput is 3--23\% lower, which is the day-to-day host drift that our \emph{relative} analysis is designed to absorb. We disclose this is a single host, so it checks temporal robustness, not cross-machine generalization.

\textbf{\S{}6.8 --- CPU/equal-CPU evidence belonged in the main text.} We added a compact main-text \textbf{resource-footprint table} (Table 5: per-layer application CPU, database CPU, combined req/CPU-second, and peak RSS on the deep fetch), with the equal-CPU one-core headline in its caption; the fuller per-metric tables remain in the supplement. To stay within the length limit we moved four secondary tables (two read patterns, aggregation, and the same-SQL control) to the supplement.
