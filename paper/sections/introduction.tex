\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Express.js remains the most widely used web framework for Node.js, and almost
every non-trivial service built on it eventually reaches a relational database
through some \emph{access layer}. The choice is not settled by the language or
the framework: access layers trade raw performance for ergonomics,
compile-time type safety, and protection from pitfalls such as the N+1 query
problem, where a naively written nested fetch silently issues one query per
parent row instead of one query for the whole result graph~\cite{chen2014orm},
a pervasive anti-pattern in real-world database-backed applications
(Section~\ref{sec:related}).
Native drivers expose the wire protocol directly (\texttt{pg} for PostgreSQL,
\texttt{mysql2} for MySQL); query builders assemble SQL programmatically
(Knex); and object-relational mappers (ORMs) map rows to objects and manage
relationships automatically, following patterns such as Data Mapper and Active
Record~\cite{fowler2002patterns} (the lightweight ORM Drizzle, together with
Prisma, Sequelize, TypeORM, Objection, and MikroORM). Node.js back-end
performance under load is itself a recognized empirical
topic~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs,kuffel2025nodejs,carmo2024backend}, but that
literature treats the runtime and web framework as the unit of comparison
rather than varying the database access layer.

In practice, the most visible performance comparisons are \emph{vendor
benchmarks}: plentiful but narrow, published by the maintainers of the
compared products, confined almost exclusively to PostgreSQL, and settling on
a single metric family rather than reporting throughput and the tail together
(Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews these in detail). None reports the p99
behavior that dominates perceived performance under
load~\cite{dean2013tail,fruth2022tail}. The peer-reviewed literature is
sparser still, limited to at most three ORMs, confined to PostgreSQL, and,
where it benchmarks PostgreSQL against MySQL directly, holding the access
layer fixed rather than varying it~\cite{colley2018orm,prisma_rawsql_2025,
orm_compare_jcct_2025,zhadko2025orm,salunke2024pgmysql} (Section~\ref{sec:related}
surveys each).

Taken together, this leaves a gap along four axes: (1)~no access-layer
comparison includes MySQL, and no PostgreSQL/MySQL benchmark varies the
access layer; (2)~no vendor-independent study spans the taxonomy broadly,
since the broad-coverage comparisons are vendor-authored and academic work
covers at most three libraries~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025,zhadko2025orm};
(3)~throughput and the p99 tail are never reported jointly, the sole vendor
suite that pairs throughput with a percentile stopping at a single P95 at one
load point~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}; and (4)~no access-layer comparison
measures client-observed performance through a production HTTP framework:
academic studies instrument query execution inside the application
process~\cite{zhadko2025orm}, and the Express-based work that does measure the
request round trip compares runtime optimizations on a single stack rather
than access layers~\cite{imamsakti2025async}.

This paper addresses that gap with a single, vendor-independent benchmark: we
serve an \emph{identical} Express application through all nine access layers
(two native drivers, the Knex query builder, and six ORMs: Drizzle, Prisma,
Sequelize, TypeORM, Objection, and MikroORM) over five access patterns chosen
to span common CRUD operations, not sampled from a measured production
workload: a point read by primary key, a keyset range scan over the most
recent rows, a deep/nested fetch materializing a post with its author and
every comment (each with its own comment-author), a per-author aggregation of
post counts and view sums, and an insert. Seven of the nine layers (all but the two engine-specific native drivers) run
against \emph{both} engines under an identical schema, dataset, and pool size;
two tuned native-driver baselines (prepared-statement variants of \texttt{pg}
and \texttt{mysql2}) bound what hand-tuned driver use achieves. Every (layer, engine, pattern) combination reports throughput (req/s) and tail
latency (p99) jointly, from 25 independent replicates. The study separates
two estimands: the \emph{default-configuration} effect of each layer used
idiomatically (RQ1--RQ3), and a controlled \emph{same-SQL} effect isolating
each layer's execution machinery from its query-generation and hydration
strategy. The full harness (adapters, seed data, an
autocannon-driven~\cite{autocannon} load runner, and an automated cross-check
that all layers return \emph{byte-identical} responses on the four
non-mutating patterns) is released as an open replication
package.\footnote{Harness, deterministic seed, all adapters, raw per-cell
measurements, and the table/figure-generating scripts:
\texttt{https://github.com/mmiotk/express-db-access-performance}; release
v1.1.0 is permanently archived at Zenodo,
\texttt{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.21339518}.}

Three research questions structure the study:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*},leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{(overhead):} How large is the throughput and tail-latency overhead of
        each access layer relative to the native-driver baseline, and how does it
        vary across the five access patterns?
  \item \emph{(engine dependence):} Is the relative ordering of access layers
        consistent across PostgreSQL and MySQL, or is it engine-dependent?
  \item \emph{(consequential patterns):} For which access patterns is the choice of
        access layer most consequential?
\end{enumerate}

This paper makes four contributions. The first two are durable; the third is
a version-dated snapshot of a fast-moving ecosystem, whereas the design and
harness outlast the pinned versions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item An \textbf{open, reproducible harness}: a uniform adapter contract, a
        deterministic seed, a matrix runner with independent replicates and
        paired request streams, a physical write-state rebuild, a same-SQL
        control with server-side protocol evidence, a
        coordinated-omission-corrected open-loop generator, and an automated
        \textbf{correctness cross-check} asserting \textbf{byte-identical}
        responses across layers on the four non-mutating patterns
        (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}).
  \item A \textbf{vendor-independent, broad-coverage benchmark design}
        spanning the taxonomy's three tiers (native driver $\to$ query
        builder $\to$ six ORMs), \textbf{dual-engine} (PostgreSQL and MySQL),
        measured at the \textbf{Express level}, reporting throughput and tail
        latency \textbf{jointly}, the combination missing from prior art.
  \item Empirical findings on the pinned versions: two not obvious in advance,
        that a \textbf{query-engine ORM can tie, and on several patterns lead,
        the native driver} at a substantial application-CPU premium (quantified
        by an equal-CPU control, Section~\ref{sec:results}), and that
        \textbf{whether writes are layer-bound depends on the engine} (visible
        only in a dual-engine design); and a controlled \textbf{confirmation
        and quantification} of the expected effects: abstraction cost
        concentrates in relation-materializing patterns and grows with the
        fetched graph, and the coarse layer ordering of reads is stable across
        engines.
  \item A practitioner-oriented \textbf{checklist of pitfalls specific to
        access-layer benchmarking} (aggregation fan-out, OFFSET versus keyset
        pagination, write-workload contamination, and query-formulation
        confounds), surfaced during development, several caught by the
        correctness cross-check and the rest by performance analysis.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper surveys related work in more detail
(Section~\ref{sec:related}), details the benchmark design
(Section~\ref{sec:methodology}), reports results per research question
(Section~\ref{sec:results}), discusses their implications
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) alongside threats to validity
(Section~\ref{sec:threats}), and concludes (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).
