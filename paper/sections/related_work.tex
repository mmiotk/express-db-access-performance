\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Peer-reviewed studies.}
Two 2025 studies benchmark Node/TypeScript access layers on PostgreSQL: one
compares Prisma against optimized raw SQL over eight query
patterns~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025} (we cite its methodology; its headline speedup
figures did not survive independent verification), and one compares Prisma,
TypeORM, and Sequelize across four relation types~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}.
Earlier, Colley et al.~\cite{colley2018orm} measured ORM overhead across eight
frameworks in Java, C\#, Python, and PHP---\emph{but not JavaScript}---making the
Node ecosystem a standing gap. On methodology, Fritz et al.~\cite{fritz2021tail}
and Dean and Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail} show why average-case reporting hides the
tail latencies that matter in production.

\paragraph{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks.}
A rich but biased landscape exists. Drizzle publishes both a Northwind micro-suite
spanning the full taxonomy (\texttt{pg}, Knex, Kysely, MikroORM, TypeORM, Prisma,
Drizzle) and an official k6-driven load benchmark reporting req/s and
P95~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's official site compares three ORMs by median
query latency but reports neither throughput nor tail percentiles~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}.
IMDBench~\cite{imdbench} and community suites (JS-AK, Orchid) add further coverage.
All share three limitations: they are PostgreSQL-only, they are authored by parties
promoting one of the compared products, and they each report a single metric family
chosen to favour a particular narrative.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
Our study differs on four axes simultaneously: (1)~it adds MySQL; (2)~it is neutral
and covers the full taxonomy in one design; (3)~it reports throughput and tail
latency jointly; and (4)~it measures through the Express HTTP layer, i.e.\ the
request roundtrip a real service experiences.
