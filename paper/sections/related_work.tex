\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We survey prior work along four \emph{coverage} axes --- taxonomy coverage, engine coverage,
joint throughput/tail reporting, and independent authorship --- along which access-layer
benchmarks vary and which our case study spans (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}); more fundamentally,
as Section~\ref{sec:related} argues, prior work leaves the comparability of its numbers
unestablished, the gap our protocol addresses. We located it with a structured scoping search (not a
systematic review) of the ACM Digital Library, IEEE Xplore, Scopus, and Google Scholar
(July~2026), crossing an access-layer set (\emph{ORM}, ``object-relational mapping'', ``query
builder'', ``database access layer'', and the seven library names) with Node.js/Express/
JavaScript, engine, and measurement terms over 2006--July~2026, followed by backward and
forward citation chaining. We retained empirical comparisons of relational access layers or
engines and excluded non-empirical, non-relational, and single-library studies; the exact
strings, per-source dates, and screening decisions are archived with the replication package
(\texttt{notes/related-work-search.md}). Because this is a scoping rather than exhaustive
search, we scope every resulting gap claim to the sources searched.

\subsection{Object-relational mapping and the access-layer spectrum}
Access layers bridge the object-relational impedance mismatch between the
relational model and the object graphs an application
manipulates~\cite{ireland2009impedance}, spanning native
drivers, query builders, and ORMs following patterns such as Data Mapper and
Active Record~\cite{fowler2002patterns} (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}).
Torres et al.~\cite{torres2017orm} survey two decades of ORM patterns; the
trade-off they document --- developer productivity against a hard-to-predict
runtime cost --- is the one this paper quantifies. The same question recurs in
the polyglot-persistence debate over non-relational
stores~\cite{sadalage2012nosql}, a choice we hold fixed while varying only the
access layer.

\subsection{ORM performance and data-access anti-patterns}
A parallel line studies ORM performance from the opposite direction, detecting and repairing
the inefficient SQL that mapping frameworks emit rather than benchmarking layers. The costliest
pattern is the N+1 query problem (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}); it and related
redundant-query anti-patterns are pervasive across real-world web
applications~\cite{yang2018dbapps,yan2017inefficiencies,shao2020antipatterns}, and their
performance impact has been quantified in ORM-backed systems~\cite{chen2016redundant,chen2014orm}
and shown often \emph{removable} by query synthesis, round-trip batching, or
caching~\cite{cheung2013querysynthesis,cheung2014sloth,chen2016cacheoptimizer} (the JavaScript
analogue being mis-sequenced \texttt{async}/\texttt{await} serializing independent
I/O~\cite{turcotte2022asyncjs}). Closest to our design, Lorenz et al.~\cite{lorenz2017orm} show
the best O/R mapping strategy depends on the database technology, van Zyl et
al.~\cite{vanzyl2009optimisations} that ORM performance is highly tuning-sensitive, and a recent
study adds an energy dimension~\cite{bonvoisin2024orm}. This literature establishes \emph{where}
ORM overhead concentrates (relation-materializing, N+1-prone access) and that it is configurable
rather than fixed, which our per-pattern results confirm; it does not compare drivers, query
builders, and ORMs head-to-head under controlled load, our aim here.

\subsection{Peer-reviewed access-layer comparisons}
The peer-reviewed literature that measures access layers head-to-head is narrow.
Colley et al.~\cite{colley2018orm} give the earliest
systematic cross-language account, benchmarking eight ORM frameworks across Java,
C\#, Python, and PHP against a common schema, but \emph{deliberately excludes
JavaScript}, leaving Node.js, and Express in particular, without an academic
baseline for the better part of a decade. Three recent studies partially address
this: one, in \emph{Procedia Computer Science}, compares Prisma against optimized
raw SQL over eight query patterns on a containerized PostgreSQL
setup~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025} (cited for its methodology; differing library
versions, hardware, workload, and harness mean we neither build on nor dispute
its absolute speedup figures); a second compares Prisma, TypeORM, and Sequelize
across four relation types, also on PostgreSQL, by response time, memory, and
CPU~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}; and the most recent instruments the same three
ORMs on PostgreSQL inside a NestJS service, timing internal query stages rather
than client-observed requests~\cite{zhadko2025orm}, independently observing the
concurrency-dependent Prisma ranking flip we also report (worst at single
queries, best under parallel load). A further Express-based study optimizes a
single stack (pooling, batching, caching) rather than comparing access
layers~\cite{imamsakti2025async}.

\subsection{Database-engine, SQL/NoSQL, and Node.js performance}
A second, largely disjoint line benchmarks the layers immediately below and above
the access layer without varying it directly. Salunke and
Ouda~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql} compare PostgreSQL and MySQL head-to-head under
standard OLTP workloads (the only dual-engine precedent found), but benchmark the
\emph{engines themselves}, bypassing any application-level access layer, so their
work cannot say how a driver, query builder, or ORM would change it; work
contrasting SQL and NoSQL databases~\cite{li2013sqlnosql} likewise measures the
store, not the access layer. On the Node.js side, Chaniotis et
al.~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs} established Node's viability as a web platform,
Kuffel and Walter~\cite{kuffel2025nodejs} tracked how its back-end performance
drifts under sustained load, and do Carmo et al.~\cite{carmo2024backend} compared
back-end frameworks more broadly. None isolates the database access layer as an
independent variable, let alone varies it across the taxonomy studied here.

\subsection{Standard benchmarks and benchmarking methodology}
Standard benchmarks define how database systems are measured: YCSB~\cite{cooper2010ycsb} for
cloud-serving stores and the TPC-C/TPC-E suites for OLTP. These target the \emph{engine} and its
transaction mix, saying nothing about the driver, query builder, or ORM this study isolates, but
they set the methodological bar for controlled, repeatable load. A separate methodological
literature explains why the vendor benchmarks' single-metric habit is itself a problem: Fruth et
al.~\cite{fruth2022tail} and Raasveldt et al.~\cite{raasveldt2018fair} catalog database-benchmarking
pitfalls (coordinated omission, warm-up, tool-induced distortion) that silently corrupt reported
percentiles; Dean and Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail} show why the tail, not the mean, determines
user-perceived latency once a request fans out, as a deep/nested fetch does; and Li et
al.~\cite{li2014tales} trace its hardware, OS, and application sources. Load-testing surveys report
such measurement is often ad hoc~\cite{jiang2015load,leitner2017exploratory} and systems results
frequently hard to reproduce~\cite{collberg2016repeatability}, motivating a shared, reusable harness
built for relevance, repeatability, and fairness~\cite{huppler2009benchmark,sim2003benchmark}. From
this literature we take a design choice absent from the access-layer benchmarks surveyed above:
report throughput \emph{and} tail latency (p99) for the same run, rather than a single metric family
chosen after the fact.

\subsection{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks}
A larger body of evidence comes from practitioners and vendors, each authored by
a party with a stake in one of the compared products. Drizzle publishes both a
Northwind micro-suite spanning nine targets (\texttt{pg}, Knex, Kysely, MikroORM,
TypeORM, Prisma, and Drizzle itself, pooled and unpooled) and an official
k6-driven load test reporting requests per second, average latency, P95, and
CPU~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's own site compares Prisma, Drizzle, and
TypeORM by median query latency over 500 iterations, reporting neither throughput
nor any latency percentile~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}. IMDBench, maintained by Gel
Data (formerly EdgeDB), adds Sequelize and node-postgres and reports both
throughput and latency on three CRUD-style operations~\cite{imdbench}. Together
these three cover more of the taxonomy than the entire peer-reviewed literature
combined, yet share the defects noted in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}: vendor
authorship, PostgreSQL-only coverage, and a single reported metric family.

\subsection{Positioning}
In the sources searched through July 2026 (protocol archived with the
replication package), the access-layer benchmarks report throughput or latency numbers whose
comparability is assumed rather than established, and none combines broad taxonomy coverage,
both engines, joint throughput/tail reporting, and independent authorship --- each covers at most
two or three of these four properties (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}). Our case study spans all
four, but the paper's contribution is the \emph{comparability protocol} it applies
(Section~\ref{sec:introduction}): the semantic-equivalence gate, the same-SQL strategy bound,
and the separated operating points that make such a comparison a controlled experiment and
that the genre otherwise leaves unaddressed --- closing the engine gap that separates
access-layer studies from engine-only work such as~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql}.
