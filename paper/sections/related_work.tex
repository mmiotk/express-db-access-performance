\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work along the same four axes on which our study differs
from it---taxonomy coverage, engine coverage, whether throughput and tail
latency are reported jointly, and the neutrality of the source---and
summarize the comparison in Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}.

\subsection{Object-relational mapping and the access-layer spectrum}
Access layers exist to bridge the object-relational impedance mismatch, the
structural gap between the row-and-table relational model and the object graphs
an application manipulates~\cite{ireland2009impedance,russell2008bridging}. They
span a spectrum from native drivers that expose SQL and the wire protocol
directly, through query builders, to object-relational mappers that hydrate rows
into objects and manage associations following patterns such as Data Mapper and
Active Record~\cite{fowler2002patterns}. Torres et al.~\cite{torres2017orm}
survey two decades of ORM patterns and their implications for application design;
the trade-off they document---developer productivity against a runtime cost that
is hard to predict---is the one this paper quantifies. The same abstraction
question recurs one level up in the polyglot-persistence debate over when to
adopt non-relational stores at all~\cite{sadalage2012nosql}; we hold that choice
fixed at relational engines and vary only the access layer.

\subsection{ORM performance and data-access anti-patterns}
A parallel line of software-engineering research studies ORM performance from the
opposite direction: rather than benchmarking layers, it detects and repairs the
inefficient SQL that mapping frameworks emit. The costliest pattern is the N+1
query problem, in which iterating over a parent collection issues one child query
per row; Yang et al.~\cite{yang2018dbapps} and Yan et al.~\cite{yan2017inefficiencies}
find it and related redundant-query anti-patterns pervasive across real-world web
applications, and Shao et al.~\cite{shao2020antipatterns} give an independent
taxonomy and detector for database-access anti-patterns. Chen et al.\ quantify the
performance impact of redundant data access in ORM-backed Java
systems~\cite{chen2016redundant}, extending their earlier anti-pattern detection
work~\cite{chen2014orm}. A complementary strand shows the overhead is often
\emph{removable}: query synthesis lifts imperative accessor code into more
efficient SQL~\cite{cheung2013querysynthesis}, lazy evaluation batches round trips
that would otherwise serialize~\cite{cheung2014sloth}, view-centric optimization
rewrites whole request handlers~\cite{yang2019view}, and configuring second-level
caching absorbs repeated reads~\cite{chen2016cacheoptimizer}; the analogous hazard
one level up, in the JavaScript runtime, is mis-sequenced \texttt{async}/\texttt{await}
that serializes independent I/O~\cite{turcotte2022asyncjs}. Closest to our design,
Lorenz et al.~\cite{lorenz2017orm} show quantitatively that the best O/R mapping
strategy depends on the underlying database technology, and van Zyl et
al.~\cite{vanzyl2009optimisations} that ORM performance is highly sensitive to
tuning, while a recent study adds an energy dimension to the picture~\cite{bonvoisin2024orm}.
This literature establishes \emph{where} ORM overhead concentrates---relation-materializing,
N+1-prone access---and that it is configurable rather than fixed; our per-pattern
results confirm and quantify both across the full layer taxonomy. It does not,
however, compare drivers, query builders, and ORMs head-to-head under controlled
load, which is our aim.

\subsection{Peer-reviewed access-layer comparisons}
The peer-reviewed literature that measures access layers head-to-head is narrow.
An early empirical study by van Zyl et al.~\cite{vanzyl2006orm} weighed object
databases against ORM tools, an ancestor of the present comparison. Colley et
al.~\cite{colley2018orm} give the earliest systematic cross-language account,
benchmarking eight ORM frameworks across Java, C\#, Python, and PHP against a
common relational schema---but the study \emph{deliberately excludes JavaScript},
leaving the Node.js ecosystem, and Express in particular, without an academic
baseline for the better part of a decade. Two 2025 studies address it partially.
One, in \emph{Procedia Computer Science}, compares Prisma against optimized raw
SQL over eight query patterns on a containerized PostgreSQL
setup~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025}; we cite its methodology, but its headline speedup
figures did not survive independent verification and are not reproduced here. The
other compares Prisma, TypeORM, and Sequelize across four relation types, also on
PostgreSQL, by response time, memory, and CPU~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}. Both
are useful precedents for containerized, Node-specific measurement, but each
covers only two or three libraries out of the much larger taxonomy in production
use, and both are confined to a single engine. No peer-reviewed study to date
spans the full native-driver/query-builder/ORM spectrum, and none includes MySQL.

\subsection{Database-engine, SQL/NoSQL, and Node.js performance}
A second, largely disjoint line of work benchmarks the layers immediately below
and above the access layer without varying it directly. Salunke and
Ouda~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql} compare PostgreSQL and MySQL head-to-head under
standard OLTP workloads---the only dual-engine precedent we found---but they
benchmark the \emph{engines themselves}, bypassing any application-level access
layer, so their work cannot say how a driver, query builder, or ORM would change
the comparison. In the broader engine-comparison line, Li and
Manoharan~\cite{li2013sqlnosql} contrast SQL and NoSQL databases and Gyorodi et
al.~\cite{gyorodi2015mongodb} compare MongoDB against MySQL specifically, while
surveys of the scalable-datastore landscape frame when each class of store
applies~\cite{cattell2011scalable,gessert2017nosql}; all measure the store, not
the application-level access layer. On the Node.js side, Chaniotis et
al.~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs} established Node's viability as a web-application
platform, Kuffel and Walter~\cite{kuffel2025nodejs} tracked how a Node.js
back-end's performance drifts over time under sustained load, do Carmo et
al.~\cite{carmo2024backend} compared back-end frameworks more broadly, and Sun et
al.~\cite{sun2019asyncgraphs} characterize how the single-threaded event loop
schedules the asynchronous I/O through which every access layer reaches the
database. None of these studies isolates the database access layer as an
independent variable, let alone varies it across the taxonomy studied here; where
database access appears at all, it is incidental to their designs rather than the
object of study.

\subsection{Standard benchmarks and benchmarking methodology}
Standard benchmarks define how database systems are normally measured:
YCSB~\cite{cooper2010ycsb} for cloud-serving stores and the TPC-C/TPC-E
transaction-processing suites~\cite{chen2011tpce} for OLTP, with
OLTP-Bench~\cite{difallah2013oltpbench} providing a reusable, extensible harness.
These target the \emph{engine} and its transaction mix; by construction they say
nothing about the driver, query builder, or ORM through which an application
issues work---the variable this study isolates---but they set the methodological
bar for controlled, repeatable load. A separate methodological literature explains
why the single-metric habit of the vendor benchmarks below is a problem in its own
right. Fruth et al.~\cite{fruth2022tail} and Raasveldt et al.~\cite{raasveldt2018fair}
catalog pitfalls specific to database benchmarking---coordinated omission,
warm-up, and tool-induced distortion---that silently corrupt reported percentiles.
Dean and Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail} show why the tail rather than the mean
determines user-perceived latency once a request fans out over many components, as
an Express request serving a deep/nested fetch does, and Li et
al.~\cite{li2014tales} trace the hardware, OS, and application sources of that
tail. Surveys of load testing~\cite{jiang2015load} and of performance-testing
practice~\cite{leitner2017exploratory} report that such measurement is often ad
hoc, and repeatability studies~\cite{collberg2016repeatability} that systems
results are frequently hard to reproduce---together motivating a shared, neutral,
reusable harness. More broadly, the benchmarking literature stresses relevance,
repeatability, and fairness as the marks of a good benchmark~\cite{huppler2009benchmark}
and the role of neutral benchmarks in advancing a field~\cite{sim2003benchmark}.
From this literature we take a design choice absent from every access-layer
benchmark surveyed above: report throughput \emph{and} tail latency (p99) for the
same run, rather than a single metric family chosen after the fact.

\subsection{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks}
A larger body of evidence comes from practitioners and vendors, though each source
is authored by a party with a stake in one of the compared products. Drizzle
publishes both a Northwind micro-suite spanning nine targets (\texttt{pg}, Knex,
Kysely, MikroORM, TypeORM, Prisma, and Drizzle itself, pooled and unpooled) and an
official k6-driven load test reporting requests per second, average latency, P95,
and CPU~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's own site compares Prisma, Drizzle, and
TypeORM by median query latency over 500 iterations, but reports neither throughput
nor any latency percentile~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}. IMDBench, maintained by Gel
Data (formerly EdgeDB), adds Sequelize and node-postgres to the mix and reports
both throughput and latency on three CRUD-style operations~\cite{imdbench}. Between
them, these three sources cover more of the access-layer taxonomy than the entire
peer-reviewed literature combined---yet all three share the same defects: each is
authored, or at minimum hosted, by a party that sells one of the compared products;
all three run exclusively against PostgreSQL; and each reports a single metric
family---throughput or latency, rarely both, and almost never the tail.

\subsection{Positioning}
No prior study, peer-reviewed or vendor, combines full-taxonomy coverage, both
engines, joint throughput/tail reporting, and neutrality; each covers at most two
or three of these four properties (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}). Our study differs
from all of them on the four axes simultaneously: (1)~it adds MySQL alongside
PostgreSQL, closing the engine gap that separates access-layer studies from
engine-only studies such as~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql}; (2)~it is neutral---the
author has no commercial stake in any compared product---and covers the full
taxonomy, native drivers, a query builder, and six ORMs, in one design, rather
than the two or three libraries typical of peer-reviewed work; (3)~it reports
throughput and tail latency (p99) jointly for every cell, rather than the single
metric family each vendor benchmark selects; and (4)~it measures through the
Express HTTP layer, i.e.\ the request round trip a real service experiences,
rather than in-process query timing alone.
