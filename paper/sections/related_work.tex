\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work along four axes on which our study differs from it ---
taxonomy coverage, engine coverage, joint throughput/tail reporting, and vendor
independence --- summarized in Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}. We located it with a
structured scoping search (not a systematic review) of the ACM Digital Library,
IEEE Xplore, Scopus, and Google Scholar (July~2026, over titles, abstracts, and
keywords), crossing an access-layer set (\emph{ORM}, ``object-relational
mapping'', ``query builder'', ``database access layer'', and the seven library
names) with context (\emph{Node.js}, \emph{Express}, \emph{JavaScript},
\emph{TypeScript}), engine (\emph{PostgreSQL}, \emph{MySQL}, ``relational
database''), and measurement (\emph{benchmark}, \emph{performance},
\emph{throughput}, \emph{latency}) sets, restricted to 2006--July~2026 and
followed by backward and forward citation chaining from the closest hits. We
retained empirical comparisons of relational access layers or engines and
excluded non-empirical, non-relational, and single-library studies. The exact
strings, per-source dates, and screening decisions are archived with the
replication package (\texttt{notes/related-work-search.md}); because this is a
scoping rather than exhaustive search, we scope every resulting gap claim to the
sources searched.

\subsection{Object-relational mapping and the access-layer spectrum}
Access layers bridge the object-relational impedance mismatch between the
relational model and the object graphs an application
manipulates~\cite{ireland2009impedance}, spanning native
drivers, query builders, and ORMs following patterns such as Data Mapper and
Active Record~\cite{fowler2002patterns} (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}).
Torres et al.~\cite{torres2017orm} survey two decades of ORM patterns; the
trade-off they document --- developer productivity against a hard-to-predict
runtime cost --- is the one this paper quantifies. The same question recurs in
the polyglot-persistence debate over non-relational
stores~\cite{sadalage2012nosql}, a choice we hold fixed while varying only the
access layer.

\subsection{ORM performance and data-access anti-patterns}
A parallel line studies ORM performance from the opposite direction, detecting
and repairing the inefficient SQL that mapping frameworks emit rather than
benchmarking layers. The costliest pattern is the N+1 query problem
(Section~\ref{sec:introduction}); it and related redundant-query anti-patterns
are pervasive across real-world web
applications~\cite{yang2018dbapps,yan2017inefficiencies}, and Shao et
al.~\cite{shao2020antipatterns} give an independent taxonomy and detector for
database-access anti-patterns. Chen et al.\ quantify the performance impact of
redundant data access in ORM-backed Java systems~\cite{chen2016redundant},
extending earlier anti-pattern detection work~\cite{chen2014orm}. A complementary
strand shows the overhead is often \emph{removable} by query synthesis, lazy
round-trip batching, or
caching~\cite{cheung2013querysynthesis,cheung2014sloth,chen2016cacheoptimizer};
the JavaScript analogue is mis-sequenced \texttt{async}/\texttt{await} serializing
independent I/O~\cite{turcotte2022asyncjs}. Closest to our design, Lorenz et
al.~\cite{lorenz2017orm} show the best O/R mapping strategy depends on the
database technology, van Zyl et al.~\cite{vanzyl2009optimisations} that ORM
performance is highly sensitive to tuning, and a recent study adds an energy
dimension~\cite{bonvoisin2024orm}. This literature establishes \emph{where} ORM
overhead concentrates (relation-materializing, N+1-prone access) and that it is
configurable rather than fixed, which our per-pattern results confirm; it does
not compare drivers, query builders, and ORMs head-to-head under controlled load,
our aim here.

\subsection{Peer-reviewed access-layer comparisons}
The peer-reviewed literature that measures access layers head-to-head is narrow.
Colley et al.~\cite{colley2018orm} give the earliest
systematic cross-language account, benchmarking eight ORM frameworks across Java,
C\#, Python, and PHP against a common schema, but \emph{deliberately excludes
JavaScript}, leaving Node.js, and Express in particular, without an academic
baseline for the better part of a decade. Three recent studies partially address
this: one, in \emph{Procedia Computer Science}, compares Prisma against optimized
raw SQL over eight query patterns on a containerized PostgreSQL
setup~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025} (cited for its methodology; differing library
versions, hardware, workload, and harness mean we neither build on nor dispute
its absolute speedup figures); a second compares Prisma, TypeORM, and Sequelize
across four relation types, also on PostgreSQL, by response time, memory, and
CPU~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}; and the most recent instruments the same three
ORMs on PostgreSQL inside a NestJS service, timing internal query stages rather
than client-observed requests~\cite{zhadko2025orm}, independently observing the
concurrency-dependent Prisma ranking flip we also report (worst at single
queries, best under parallel load). A further Express-based study optimizes a
single stack (pooling, batching, caching) rather than comparing access
layers~\cite{imamsakti2025async}.

\subsection{Database-engine, SQL/NoSQL, and Node.js performance}
A second, largely disjoint line benchmarks the layers immediately below and above
the access layer without varying it directly. Salunke and
Ouda~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql} compare PostgreSQL and MySQL head-to-head under
standard OLTP workloads (the only dual-engine precedent found), but benchmark the
\emph{engines themselves}, bypassing any application-level access layer, so their
work cannot say how a driver, query builder, or ORM would change it; work
contrasting SQL and NoSQL databases~\cite{li2013sqlnosql} likewise measures the
store, not the access layer. On the Node.js side, Chaniotis et
al.~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs} established Node's viability as a web platform,
Kuffel and Walter~\cite{kuffel2025nodejs} tracked how its back-end performance
drifts under sustained load, and do Carmo et al.~\cite{carmo2024backend} compared
back-end frameworks more broadly. None isolates the database access layer as an
independent variable, let alone varies it across the taxonomy studied here.

\subsection{Standard benchmarks and benchmarking methodology}
Standard benchmarks define how database systems are measured: YCSB~\cite{cooper2010ycsb}
for cloud-serving stores and the TPC-C/TPC-E suites for OLTP. These target the
\emph{engine} and its transaction mix, saying nothing about the driver, query builder, or ORM through
which an application issues work (the variable this study isolates), but they
set the methodological bar for controlled, repeatable load. A separate
methodological literature explains why the vendor benchmarks' single-metric
habit is a problem in its own right: Fruth et al.~\cite{fruth2022tail} and
Raasveldt et al.~\cite{raasveldt2018fair} catalog pitfalls specific to database
benchmarking (coordinated omission, warm-up, tool-induced distortion) that
silently corrupt reported percentiles; Dean and Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail}
show why the tail, not the mean, determines user-perceived latency once a
request fans out over many components, as an Express request serving a
deep/nested fetch does; and Li et al.~\cite{li2014tales} trace the hardware,
OS, and application sources of that tail. Load-testing surveys report such measurement is often
ad hoc~\cite{jiang2015load,leitner2017exploratory}, and repeatability studies
find systems results frequently hard to reproduce~\cite{collberg2016repeatability},
motivating a shared, reusable harness; the benchmarking literature stresses
relevance, repeatability, and fairness~\cite{huppler2009benchmark}
and the role of neutral benchmarks in advancing a field~\cite{sim2003benchmark}. From
this literature we take a design choice absent from every access-layer
benchmark surveyed above: report throughput \emph{and} tail latency (p99) for
the same run, rather than a single metric family chosen after the fact.

\subsection{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks}
A larger body of evidence comes from practitioners and vendors, each authored by
a party with a stake in one of the compared products. Drizzle publishes both a
Northwind micro-suite spanning nine targets (\texttt{pg}, Knex, Kysely, MikroORM,
TypeORM, Prisma, and Drizzle itself, pooled and unpooled) and an official
k6-driven load test reporting requests per second, average latency, P95, and
CPU~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's own site compares Prisma, Drizzle, and
TypeORM by median query latency over 500 iterations, reporting neither throughput
nor any latency percentile~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}. IMDBench, maintained by Gel
Data (formerly EdgeDB), adds Sequelize and node-postgres and reports both
throughput and latency on three CRUD-style operations~\cite{imdbench}. Together
these three cover more of the taxonomy than the entire peer-reviewed literature
combined, yet share the defects noted in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}: vendor
authorship, PostgreSQL-only coverage, and a single reported metric family.

\subsection{Positioning}
In the sources searched through July 2026 (protocol archived with the
replication package), no study combined broad taxonomy coverage, both engines,
joint throughput/tail reporting, and vendor independence; each covers at most two
or three of these four properties (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}). This study
combines all four (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}), closing the same engine gap
separating access-layer studies from engine-only work such
as~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql}.
