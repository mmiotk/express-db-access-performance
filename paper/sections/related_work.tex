\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We survey prior work along four \emph{coverage} axes --- taxonomy coverage, engine coverage,
joint throughput/tail reporting, and vendor involvement --- along which access-layer
benchmarks vary and which our case study spans (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}); more fundamentally,
prior work leaves the comparability of its numbers unestablished, the gap our protocol addresses.
We located it with a structured scoping search (not a systematic review) of the ACM Digital Library,
IEEE Xplore, Scopus, and Google Scholar (July~2026), crossing access-layer terms (ORM, query builder,
the seven library names) with Node.js, engine, and measurement terms over 2006--July~2026, then
backward and forward citation chaining. We retained empirical comparisons of relational access layers
or engines and excluded non-empirical, non-relational, and single-library studies; the exact strings,
per-source dates, and screening decisions are archived with the replication package
(\texttt{notes/related-work-search.md}). Because this is a scoping rather than exhaustive
search, we scope every resulting gap claim to the sources searched.

\subsection{Object-relational mapping and the access-layer spectrum}
Access layers bridge the object-relational impedance mismatch between the
relational model and the object graphs an application
manipulates~\cite{ireland2009impedance}, spanning native
drivers, query builders, and ORMs following patterns such as Data Mapper and
Active Record~\cite{fowler2002patterns} (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}).
Torres et al.~\cite{torres2017orm} survey two decades of ORM patterns; the
trade-off they document --- developer productivity against a hard-to-predict
runtime cost --- is the one this paper quantifies, holding the store choice
(relational vs.\ non-relational~\cite{sadalage2012nosql}) fixed while varying only the access layer.

\subsection{ORM performance and data-access anti-patterns}
A parallel line studies ORM performance from the opposite direction, detecting and repairing
the inefficient SQL that mapping frameworks emit rather than benchmarking layers. The costliest
pattern is the N+1 query problem (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}); it and related
redundant-query anti-patterns are pervasive across real-world web
applications~\cite{yang2018dbapps,yan2017inefficiencies,shao2020antipatterns}, and their
performance impact has been quantified in ORM-backed systems~\cite{chen2016redundant,chen2014orm}
and shown often \emph{removable} by query synthesis, round-trip batching, or
caching~\cite{cheung2013querysynthesis,cheung2014sloth,chen2016cacheoptimizer} (with a JavaScript
analogue in mis-sequenced \texttt{async}/\texttt{await}~\cite{turcotte2022asyncjs}). Closest to our design, Lorenz et al.~\cite{lorenz2017orm} show
the best O/R mapping strategy depends on the database technology, van Zyl et
al.~\cite{vanzyl2009optimisations} that ORM performance is highly tuning-sensitive, and a recent
study adds an energy dimension~\cite{bonvoisin2024orm}. This literature establishes \emph{where}
ORM overhead concentrates and that it is configurable, not fixed --- consistent with our per-pattern
results in this configuration --- but does not compare drivers, query builders, and ORMs head-to-head
under controlled load, our aim here.

\subsection{Peer-reviewed access-layer comparisons}
The peer-reviewed literature that measures access layers head-to-head is narrow.
Colley et al.~\cite{colley2018orm} give the earliest
systematic cross-language account, benchmarking eight ORM frameworks across Java,
C\#, Python, and PHP against a common schema, but \emph{deliberately excludes
JavaScript}, leaving it without an academic baseline for nearly a decade. Three recent studies partially address
this: one, in \emph{Procedia Computer Science}, compares Prisma against optimized
raw SQL over eight query patterns on a containerized PostgreSQL
setup~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025} (differing versions, hardware, and workload, so we
neither build on nor dispute its figures); a second compares Prisma, TypeORM, and Sequelize
across four relation types, also on PostgreSQL, by response time, memory, and
CPU~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}; and the most recent instruments the same three
ORMs on PostgreSQL inside a NestJS service, timing internal query stages rather
than client-observed requests~\cite{zhadko2025orm}, independently observing the
concurrency-dependent Prisma ranking flip we also report. A further Express-based study optimizes a
single stack (pooling, batching, caching) rather than comparing access
layers~\cite{imamsakti2025async}.

\subsection{Database-engine, SQL/NoSQL, and Node.js performance}
A second, largely disjoint line benchmarks the layers immediately below and above
the access layer without varying it directly. Salunke and
Ouda~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql} compare PostgreSQL and MySQL head-to-head under
standard OLTP workloads (the only dual-engine precedent found), but benchmark the
\emph{engines themselves}, so they cannot say how an access layer would change it;
SQL/NoSQL comparisons~\cite{li2013sqlnosql} likewise measure the store, not the layer. On the Node.js side, prior work establishes Node's web
viability~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs}, tracks its back-end performance drift under sustained
load~\cite{kuffel2025nodejs}, and compares back-end frameworks broadly~\cite{carmo2024backend}, but
none treats the database access layer as an independent variable, let alone varies it across the
taxonomy studied here.

\subsection{Standard benchmarks and benchmarking methodology}
Standard benchmarks define how database systems are measured: YCSB~\cite{cooper2010ycsb} for
cloud-serving stores and the TPC-C/TPC-E suites for OLTP. These target the \emph{engine}, saying
nothing about the driver, query builder, or ORM this study varies, but set the bar for controlled,
repeatable load. A separate methodological literature explains why single-metric reporting is a
problem: Fruth et al.~\cite{fruth2022tail} and Raasveldt et al.~\cite{raasveldt2018fair} catalog
database-benchmarking pitfalls (coordinated omission, warm-up, tool-induced distortion); Dean and
Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail} show the tail, not the mean, determines user-perceived latency under
fan-out; and Li et al.~\cite{li2014tales} trace its sources. Such measurement is often ad
hoc~\cite{jiang2015load,leitner2017exploratory} and hard to
reproduce~\cite{collberg2016repeatability}, motivating a reusable harness built for relevance,
repeatability, and fairness~\cite{huppler2009benchmark,sim2003benchmark}. From it we take a design
choice absent from the access-layer benchmarks above: report throughput \emph{and} p99 for the same
run, not a single metric chosen after the fact.

\subsection{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks}
A larger body of evidence comes from practitioners and vendors, each authored by
a party with a stake in one of the compared products. Drizzle publishes a
Northwind micro-suite (nine targets, pooled and unpooled) and an official k6 load test
reporting req/s, average latency, P95, and CPU~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's own site compares
Prisma, Drizzle, and TypeORM by median query latency over 500 iterations, reporting neither throughput
nor any latency percentile~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}. IMDBench adds Sequelize and node-postgres,
reporting throughput and latency on three CRUD operations~\cite{imdbench}. Together
these three cover more of the taxonomy than the entire peer-reviewed literature
combined, yet share the defects noted in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}: vendor
authorship, PostgreSQL-only coverage, and a single reported metric family.

\subsection{Positioning}
In the sources searched through July 2026 the access-layer benchmarks report numbers whose
comparability is assumed rather than established, and we did not identify a study combining broad
taxonomy coverage, both engines, joint throughput/tail reporting, and independent authorship --- each
covers at most two or three (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}) --- the strongest claim the scoping search
supports, not a claim of priority. But coverage is not the contribution, and methodological novelty is
a separate question: which existing benchmark-quality or experimental-design framework, applied
carefully, would already \emph{generate} this protocol? In the sources searched, none. Kounev et al.'s
systems-benchmarking criteria~\cite{kounev2020benchmarking}, Hasselbring's software-engineering
benchmarking standard~\cite{hasselbring2021benchmarking}, and Raasveldt and M\"uhleisen's database
fair-benchmarking guidance~\cite{raasveldt2018fair} supply the principles it rests on --- fairness,
verifiability, experimental design, validate-then-report --- but operationalize neither of its
access-layer constructs:
none prescribes a documentation-selected, predeclared rule for \emph{what code represents a library} or
a same-SQL contrast holding the query strategy fixed, and their correctness norms (``verify results'',
construct validity) stop short of a per-cell admission gate requiring byte-identical responses and
verified write-state \emph{before} timing. Correctness-before-timing has precedents (SPEC output
validation, TPC ACID, SQLancer's equivalence oracles~\cite{rigger2020sqlancer}), but each validates one
program or engine against its own reference, not equivalence \emph{across competing implementations of
one task} as an admission precondition. That joint operationalization for access layers
(Section~\ref{sec:introduction}), not the coverage, is the methodological novelty; a pre-submission
search past the July~2026 freeze surfaced no new access-layer prior art.
