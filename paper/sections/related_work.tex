\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work along four axes on which our study differs from it
(taxonomy coverage, engine coverage, whether throughput and tail latency are
reported jointly, and vendor independence), summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}. We located this work by searching the ACM Digital
Library, IEEE Xplore, Scopus, and Google Scholar for combinations of
\emph{ORM}, \emph{query builder}, \emph{database access layer}, \emph{Node.js},
\emph{Express}, \emph{PostgreSQL}, and \emph{MySQL} with \emph{benchmark},
\emph{performance}, \emph{throughput}, and \emph{latency} over 2006--July 2026,
followed by citation chaining, retaining empirical performance comparisons of
relational access layers or engines and setting aside non-empirical or
non-relational studies.

\subsection{Object-relational mapping and the access-layer spectrum}
Access layers exist to bridge the object-relational impedance mismatch between
the row-and-table relational model and the object graphs an application
manipulates~\cite{ireland2009impedance,russell2008bridging}, spanning native
drivers, query builders, and object-relational mappers that hydrate rows into
objects and manage associations following patterns such as Data Mapper and
Active Record~\cite{fowler2002patterns} (Section~\ref{sec:introduction}).
Torres et al.~\cite{torres2017orm} survey two decades of ORM patterns and their
implications for application design; the trade-off they document, developer
productivity against a runtime cost that is hard to predict, is the one this
paper quantifies. The same abstraction question recurs one level up in the
polyglot-persistence debate over whether to adopt non-relational stores at
all~\cite{sadalage2012nosql}; we hold that choice fixed at relational engines
and vary only the access layer.

\subsection{ORM performance and data-access anti-patterns}
A parallel line of research studies ORM performance from the opposite
direction: rather than benchmarking layers, it detects and repairs the
inefficient SQL that mapping frameworks emit. The costliest pattern is the N+1
query problem, where iterating over a parent collection issues one child query
per row; it and related redundant-query anti-patterns are pervasive across
real-world web applications~\cite{yang2018dbapps,yan2017inefficiencies}, and
Shao et al.~\cite{shao2020antipatterns} give an independent taxonomy and
detector for database-access anti-patterns. Chen et
al.\ quantify the performance impact of redundant data access in ORM-backed
Java systems~\cite{chen2016redundant}, extending earlier anti-pattern detection
work~\cite{chen2014orm}. A complementary strand shows the overhead is often
\emph{removable}: query synthesis lifts imperative accessor code into more
efficient SQL~\cite{cheung2013querysynthesis}, lazy evaluation batches round
trips that would otherwise serialize~\cite{cheung2014sloth}, view-centric
optimization rewrites whole request handlers~\cite{yang2019view}, and
second-level caching absorbs repeated reads~\cite{chen2016cacheoptimizer}; the
analogous hazard in the JavaScript runtime is mis-sequenced
\texttt{async}/\texttt{await} that serializes independent
I/O~\cite{turcotte2022asyncjs}. Closest to our design, Lorenz et
al.~\cite{lorenz2017orm} show quantitatively that the best O/R mapping strategy
depends on the underlying database technology, van Zyl et
al.~\cite{vanzyl2009optimisations} that ORM performance is highly sensitive to
tuning, and a recent study adds an energy dimension~\cite{bonvoisin2024orm}.
This literature establishes \emph{where} ORM overhead concentrates
(relation-materializing, N+1-prone access) and that it is configurable rather
than fixed; our per-pattern results confirm and quantify both across the
layers studied here, but it does not compare drivers, query builders, and ORMs
head-to-head under controlled load, which is our aim.

\subsection{Peer-reviewed access-layer comparisons}
The peer-reviewed literature that measures access layers head-to-head is
narrow. An early study by van Zyl et al.~\cite{vanzyl2006orm} weighed object
databases against ORM tools, an ancestor of the present comparison.
Colley et al.~\cite{colley2018orm} give the earliest systematic cross-language
account, benchmarking eight ORM frameworks across Java, C\#, Python, and PHP
against a common relational schema, but \emph{deliberately excludes
JavaScript}, leaving the Node.js ecosystem, and Express in particular, without
an academic baseline for the better part of a decade. Three recent studies partially address this: one, in \emph{Procedia
Computer Science}, compares Prisma against optimized raw SQL over eight query
patterns on a containerized PostgreSQL setup~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025} (cited
for its methodology; because the library versions, hardware, workload, and
harness differ from ours, we neither build on nor dispute its absolute speedup
figures); a second compares Prisma, TypeORM,
and Sequelize across four relation types, also on PostgreSQL, by response
time, memory, and CPU~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}; and the most recent
instruments the same three ORMs on PostgreSQL inside a NestJS service, timing
internal query stages rather than client-observed
requests~\cite{zhadko2025orm}, independently observing the
concurrency-dependent Prisma ranking flip we also report (worst at single
queries, best under parallel load). All three are
useful precedents for containerized, Node-specific measurement, but each
covers at most three libraries, each is confined to PostgreSQL, and none
measures the client-observed HTTP round trip. A further Express-based study optimizes a single stack
(pooling, batching, caching) rather than comparing access
layers~\cite{imamsakti2025async}. None of the peer-reviewed work located spans the native-driver,
query-builder, and ORM tiers together, or includes MySQL.

\subsection{Database-engine, SQL/NoSQL, and Node.js performance}
A second, largely disjoint line of work benchmarks the layers immediately
below and above the access layer without varying it directly. Salunke and
Ouda~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql} compare PostgreSQL and MySQL head-to-head
under standard OLTP workloads (the only dual-engine precedent found), but
benchmark the \emph{engines themselves}, bypassing any application-level
access layer, so their work cannot say how a driver, query builder, or ORM
would change it. In the broader engine-comparison line, Li and Manoharan~\cite{li2013sqlnosql}
contrast SQL and NoSQL databases, Gyorodi et al.~\cite{gyorodi2015mongodb}
compare MongoDB against MySQL, and surveys of the scalable-datastore landscape
frame when each class of store applies~\cite{cattell2011scalable,gessert2017nosql};
all measure the store, not the access layer. On the Node.js side, Chaniotis et
al.~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs} established Node's viability as a
web-application platform, Kuffel and Walter~\cite{kuffel2025nodejs} tracked how
Node.js back-end performance drifts under sustained load, do Carmo et
al.~\cite{carmo2024backend} compared back-end frameworks more broadly, and Sun
et al.~\cite{sun2019asyncgraphs} characterize how the single-threaded event
loop schedules the asynchronous I/O every access layer relies on. None
isolates the database access layer as an independent variable, let alone
varies it across the taxonomy studied here; where database access appears, it
is incidental to their designs rather than the object of study.

\subsection{Standard benchmarks and benchmarking methodology}
Standard benchmarks define how database systems are normally measured:
YCSB~\cite{cooper2010ycsb} for cloud-serving stores and the TPC-C/TPC-E
suites~\cite{chen2011tpce} for OLTP, with OLTP-Bench~\cite{difallah2013oltpbench}
providing a reusable, extensible harness. These target the \emph{engine} and its
transaction mix, saying nothing about the driver, query builder, or ORM through
which an application issues work (the variable this study isolates), but they
set the methodological bar for controlled, repeatable load. A separate
methodological literature explains why the vendor benchmarks' single-metric
habit is a problem in its own right: Fruth et al.~\cite{fruth2022tail} and
Raasveldt et al.~\cite{raasveldt2018fair} catalog pitfalls specific to database
benchmarking (coordinated omission, warm-up, tool-induced distortion) that
silently corrupt reported percentiles; Dean and Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail}
show why the tail, not the mean, determines user-perceived latency once a
request fans out over many components, as an Express request serving a
deep/nested fetch does; and Li et al.~\cite{li2014tales} trace the hardware,
OS, and application sources of that tail. Load-testing and performance-testing surveys report such measurement is often
ad hoc~\cite{jiang2015load,leitner2017exploratory}, and repeatability studies
find systems results frequently hard to reproduce~\cite{collberg2016repeatability},
motivating a shared, reusable harness; the benchmarking literature more
broadly stresses relevance, repeatability, and fairness~\cite{huppler2009benchmark}
and the role of neutral benchmarks in advancing a field~\cite{sim2003benchmark}. From
this literature we take a design choice absent from every access-layer
benchmark surveyed above: report throughput \emph{and} tail latency (p99) for
the same run, rather than a single metric family chosen after the fact.

\subsection{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks}
A larger body of evidence comes from practitioners and vendors, though each
source is authored by a party with a stake in one of the compared products.
Drizzle publishes both a Northwind micro-suite spanning nine targets
(\texttt{pg}, Knex, Kysely, MikroORM, TypeORM, Prisma, and Drizzle itself,
pooled and unpooled) and an official k6-driven load test reporting requests per
second, average latency, P95, and CPU~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's own
site compares Prisma, Drizzle, and TypeORM by median query latency over 500
iterations, reporting neither throughput nor any latency
percentile~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}. IMDBench, maintained by Gel Data (formerly
EdgeDB), adds Sequelize and node-postgres and reports both throughput and
latency on three CRUD-style operations~\cite{imdbench}. Together these three sources cover more of the taxonomy than the entire
peer-reviewed literature combined, yet share the same defects: each is
authored, or at least hosted, by a party selling one of the compared
products; all run exclusively against PostgreSQL; and each reports a single
metric family, throughput or latency rarely both, almost never the tail.

\subsection{Positioning}
In the sources searched through July 2026 (search protocol archived with the
replication package), no study combined broad taxonomy coverage, both
engines, joint throughput/tail reporting, and vendor independence; each work
found covers at most two or three of these four properties
(Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}). This study combines all four: it adds MySQL
alongside PostgreSQL, spans a representative native-driver-to-ORM
cross-section rather than the two or three libraries typical of peer-reviewed
work, is authored by no evaluated vendor, reports throughput and response-time
p99 jointly for every cell, and measures through the Express HTTP layer
rather than in-process query timing alone, closing the same engine gap that
separates access-layer studies from engine-only work
such as~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql}.
