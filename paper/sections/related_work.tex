\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize prior work along the same four axes on which our study differs
from it---taxonomy coverage, engine coverage, whether throughput and tail
latency are reported jointly, and the neutrality of the source---and
summarize the comparison in Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}.

\paragraph{Peer-reviewed access-layer and ORM studies.}
The peer-reviewed literature on access-layer performance is recent, active,
and narrow. Colley et al.~\cite{colley2018orm} give the earliest systematic
account, benchmarking eight ORM frameworks across Java, C\#, Python, and PHP
against a common relational schema---but the study \emph{deliberately
excludes JavaScript}, leaving the Node.js ecosystem, and Express in
particular, without an academic baseline for the better part of a decade.
Two 2025 studies begin to close that gap, but only partially. One, in
\emph{Procedia Computer Science}, compares Prisma against optimized raw SQL
over eight query patterns on a containerized PostgreSQL
setup~\cite{prisma_rawsql_2025}; we cite its methodology, but its headline
speedup figures did not survive independent verification and are not
reproduced here. The other compares Prisma, TypeORM, and Sequelize across
four relation types, also on PostgreSQL, by response time, memory, and
CPU~\cite{orm_compare_jcct_2025}. Both are useful precedents for
containerized, Node-specific measurement, but each covers only two or three
libraries out of the much larger taxonomy in production use, and both are
confined to a single engine. No peer-reviewed study to date spans the full
native-driver/query-builder/ORM spectrum, and none includes MySQL.

\paragraph{Database-engine and Node.js performance studies.}
A second, largely disjoint line of work benchmarks the layers immediately
below and above the access layer without varying it directly. Salunke and
Ouda~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql} compare PostgreSQL and MySQL head-to-head
under standard OLTP workloads---the only dual-engine precedent we
found---but they benchmark the \emph{engines themselves}, bypassing any
application-level access layer; their work is complementary to ours but
orthogonal, since it cannot say how a driver, query builder, or ORM would
change the comparison. On the Node.js side, Chaniotis et
al.~\cite{chaniotis2015nodejs} established Node's viability as a
web-application platform, Kuffel and Walter~\cite{kuffel2025nodejs} tracked
how a Node.js backend's performance characteristics drift over time under
sustained load, and do Carmo et al.~\cite{carmo2024backend} compared
back-end frameworks more broadly. None of these studies isolates the
database access layer as an independent variable, let alone varies it across
the taxonomy studied here; where database access appears at all, it is
incidental to their designs rather than the object of study.

\paragraph{Practitioner and vendor benchmarks.}
A far richer, but far less trustworthy, body of evidence comes from
practitioners and vendors. Drizzle publishes both a Northwind micro-suite
spanning nine targets (\texttt{pg}, Knex, Kysely, MikroORM, TypeORM, Prisma,
and Drizzle itself, pooled and unpooled) and an official k6-driven load test
reporting requests per second, average latency, P95, and
CPU~\cite{drizzle_benchmarks}. Prisma's own site compares Prisma, Drizzle,
and TypeORM by median query latency over 500 iterations, but reports
neither throughput nor any latency percentile~\cite{prisma_benchmarks}.
IMDBench, maintained by Gel Data (formerly EdgeDB), adds Sequelize and
node-postgres to the mix and reports both throughput and latency on three
CRUD-style operations~\cite{imdbench}. Between them, these three sources
cover more of the access-layer taxonomy than the entire peer-reviewed
literature combined---yet all three share the same defects: each is
authored, or at minimum hosted, by a party that sells one of the compared
products; all three run exclusively against PostgreSQL; and each reports a
single metric family---throughput or latency, rarely both, and almost never
the tail---chosen in a way that happens to flatter its author's product.

\paragraph{Benchmarking methodology.}
A separate methodological literature explains why the single-metric habit
above is a problem in its own right. Fritz et al.~\cite{fritz2021tail}
catalog pitfalls specific to database benchmarking---coordinated omission,
JIT and garbage-collector warm-up, and tool-induced distortion---that
silently corrupt reported p95/p99 values unless explicitly controlled for.
Dean and Barroso~\cite{dean2013tail} show, at data-center scale, why the
tail rather than the mean determines user-perceived latency once a request
fans out over many components, as an Express request serving a deep/nested
fetch does. Neither paper studies access layers, but together they motivate
a design choice absent from every benchmark surveyed above: report
throughput \emph{and} tail latency (p99) for the same run, rather
than a single metric chosen after the fact.

\paragraph{Positioning.}
No prior study, peer-reviewed or vendor, combines full-taxonomy coverage,
both engines, joint throughput/tail reporting, and neutrality; each covers
at most two or three of these four properties (Table~\ref{tab:prior_art}).
Our study differs from all of them on the four axes simultaneously:
(1)~it adds MySQL alongside PostgreSQL, closing the engine gap that
separates access-layer studies from engine-only studies such
as~\cite{salunke2024pgmysql}; (2)~it is neutral---the author has no
commercial stake in any compared product---and covers the full taxonomy,
native drivers, a query builder, and six ORMs, in one design, rather than
the two or three libraries typical of peer-reviewed work; (3)~it reports
throughput and tail latency (p99) jointly for every cell, rather
than the single metric family each vendor benchmark selects; and (4)~it
measures through the Express HTTP layer, i.e.\ the request roundtrip a real
service experiences, rather than in-process query timing alone.
