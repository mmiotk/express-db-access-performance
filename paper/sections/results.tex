\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The tables below are generated directly by the harness and report throughput
(requests per second, higher is better) and tail latency (p99, milliseconds, lower
is better) per access layer and engine, one table per access pattern. All numbers
come from the run described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology} (seed of 2{,}000
authors, 100{,}000 posts and 1{,}000{,}000 comments; 50 concurrent connections;
median of ten measured runs after a warm-up pass; connection pool fixed at ten).
Absolute throughput is hardware-specific; the comparison of interest is
\emph{relative} across layers within an engine.

\subsection{RQ1: overhead relative to the native baseline}
The native \texttt{pg} driver leads on every PostgreSQL pattern, but the cost of
abstraction is strongly \emph{pattern-dependent} (Tables~\ref{tab:point_read_rps},
\ref{tab:deep_fetch_rps}, \ref{tab:aggregation_rps}). On the \textbf{deep/nested
fetch}---the pattern that assembles a post, its author, and all comments with their
authors---Prisma reaches 3{,}569~req/s against native's 3{,}616, a gap of only a
few percent, and Knex (2{,}454) and the lightweight ORM Drizzle (2{,}098) remain
competitive; in contrast the data-mapper ORMs fall away sharply---TypeORM 1{,}259,
Sequelize 1{,}066, Objection 1{,}009, and MikroORM just 579, i.e.\ $6.2\times$ below
native. \textbf{Point reads} show a milder $3.2\times$ spread (native 6{,}902 down
to MikroORM 2{,}141). \textbf{Aggregation} is nearly layer-agnostic: because every
layer issues the same correlated-subquery plan through its raw-SQL path, throughput
ranges only $1.8\times$ (native 7{,}210; Prisma is fastest at 8{,}181; Objection
slowest at 4{,}047). The picture is that abstraction is cheap when the layer merely
forwards a query and expensive when it must materialize a nested object graph.

\subsection{RQ2: engine dependence of the ranking}
The \emph{relative} ordering of access layers is largely stable across PostgreSQL
and MySQL: native drivers and Prisma lead, query builders and the lightweight ORM
form a middle band, and the data-mapper ORMs trail, on both engines (the
deep-fetch ordering, for instance, is preserved, with a $4.5\times$ spread on
MySQL versus $6.2\times$ on PostgreSQL). The clearest engine-specific effect is on
\textbf{inserts} (Table~\ref{tab:write_rps}): on MySQL, throughput is uniformly low
across \emph{all} layers---roughly 750--930~req/s, a spread of only $1.2\times$---so
the InnoDB commit path dominates and masks access-layer differences, whereas on
PostgreSQL the same pattern spreads $4.0\times$ (native 6{,}375 down to MikroORM
1{,}575). Thus for writes the engine, not the access layer, is the primary
determinant of throughput. The keyset range scan is healthy on both engines
(Table~\ref{tab:range_scan_rps}; native \texttt{mysql2} 2{,}901, \texttt{pg}
3{,}620), confirming that the earlier collapse we observed under \texttt{OFFSET}
pagination was a workload artefact rather than an engine or layer property
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{RQ3: which patterns matter most}
Ranking the patterns by how much the layer choice moves throughput yields a clear
order. The deep/nested fetch is the most consequential ($6.2\times$ on PostgreSQL,
$4.5\times$ on MySQL), followed by inserts on PostgreSQL ($4.0\times$; but only
$1.2\times$ on MySQL, where the engine dominates) and point reads ($3.2\times$ /
$2.4\times$). Aggregation is the least consequential once every layer uses the
raw-SQL escape hatch ($1.8\times$ / $1.4\times$). In short, the more of the result
graph the access layer must assemble on the application side, the more the choice
of layer costs.

\subsection{Tail latency}
Tail latency tracks throughput: the layers and patterns that lose throughput also
exhibit the worst p99 (Tables~\ref{tab:deep_fetch_p99}, \ref{tab:range_scan_p99}).
The data-mapper ORMs' deep-fetch cells, in particular, combine the lowest
throughput with the highest p99, so the penalty a heavyweight ORM imposes on a
nested-fetch hot path is visible not only in mean capacity but in the tail that
governs user-perceived latency at scale---precisely the quantity that
single-metric, throughput-only vendor benchmarks omit.

\subsection{Measurement stability and significance}
The rankings above rest on stable measurements. Across all 80 cells the coefficient
of variation of throughput over the ten runs is at most 10.7\%
(Table~\ref{tab:cv}), an order of magnitude below the between-layer spreads
(up to $6.2\times$). To confirm that neighbouring ranks are not measurement
artifacts, we compare each adjacently ranked pair on the deep/nested fetch
(Table~\ref{tab:significance}): \emph{every} adjacent pair separates with a large
Cliff's~$\delta$ effect size ($\delta \geq 0.66$) and a Mann--Whitney~$U$
$p \leq 0.014$, including the closest pairs (e.g.\ Sequelize $>$ Objection,
1{,}066 vs.\ 1{,}009~req/s, $\delta = 0.78$, $p = 0.004$). The full ordering---%
native, Prisma, the query builder and lightweight ORM, then the data-mapper
ORMs---is therefore statistically supported at every step, not merely in aggregate.

\subsection{Scaling with concurrency}
Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} sweeps the deep/nested fetch from 1 to 256 concurrent
connections. Two observations hold across the load range: the relative ordering of
layers is preserved at every concurrency level (the curves do not cross), and the
lightweight layers saturate at a higher throughput ceiling than the data-mapper
ORMs. This shows the RQ1 finding is not an artifact of the single 50-connection
operating point but a property of the layers across load.

\subsection{Resource footprint}
Throughput is not the only cost (Table~\ref{tab:resources}). The most striking
observation is that Prisma's near-native throughput on the deep/nested fetch is
bought with roughly \textbf{five times the CPU}: its Node.js process draws
$485\%$---close to five saturated cores---against the $\approx100\%$ (one core)
of every other layer, because Prisma delegates query execution to a separate
engine and worker threads. What reads as ``ORM parity with native'' in throughput
is thus a trade of cores for latency, invisible to a throughput-only benchmark.
Memory tells a gentler story: the native driver, Knex, and Objection are lightest
(204--213~MB peak RSS), while the remaining ORMs sit higher (251--278~MB). Resource
cost, like throughput, is therefore pattern- and layer-specific, and matters when
sizing horizontally scaled deployments.
